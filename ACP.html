Snakes of Bangalore

A short list showcasing some of the diverse snake species seen in the Bangalore Urban District.
These species are displayed by family-level taxonomy.
Though only 8 species are showcased here, a recent study from 2021 shows that a total of 33 species are found within the district. Of which, only 4 are medically significant (life threatening) to humans.

Vipers (Viperidae)

All Vipers are venomous and are generally characterised by their keeled scales, triangular shaped heads, and slit-shaped pupils. Vipers typically have a stocky build, suiting them as nocturnal ambush predators. Unlike most snakes, most vipers are ovoviviparous, meaning they give live birth as opposed to laying eggs.

Russell's Viper (Daboia russelii) - Venomous

Russell's Vipers are part of the "Big 4" species of venomous snakes found in India and are responsible for the most amount of snakebite deaths in Asia. These snakes thrive in agricultural areas and most bites happen when people walk around outside without a light and acidentally step on the snake.
Although they seem menacing, these snakes don't want to hurt humans and will let out a loud warning hiss that sounds like a pressure-cooker.


Identification Methods:
  • Yellow-brown coloured body with a chain-pattern outlined by black.
  • Slow moving, robust body with keeled scales.
Sometimes confused with:
  • Indian Rock Python
  • Common Sand Boa

Saw-scaled Viper (Echis carinatus) - Venomous

Saw-scaled Vipers are part of the "Big 4" and get their name from the sawing sound their keeled scales make when rubbing together in an S-shaped motion. They make this sound as a warning. These snakes are generally quite small in size (15 - 30 inches) but have one of the fastest strikes.


Identification Methods:
  • Reddish-brown coloured body with zig-zag pattern and a '+' shaped pattern on the head.
  • Small but stout body with keeled scales and stubby tail.
Sometimes confused with:
  • Common Cat Snake



Elapids (Elapidae)

Practically all Elapids are venomous, characterised by typically having smooth scales, slender bodies, and round pupils. All elapids have front fangs that are fixed in place and inject primarily neurotoxic venom. Most are oviparous, meaning they lay eggs rather than giving birth.

Spectacled Cobra (Naja naja) - Venomous

Spectacled Cobras are part of the "Big 4", famous for the spectacle pattern on the back of its hood. These cobras also have two dots on the front side of the hood that serve as fake eyes to distract predators.
Cobras can raise up to 1/3 of their body and "hood up" by flattening their neck ribs, stretching the skin out as a threatening display.
Though they have a potent neurotoxic venom (drop-for-drop more potent than the King Cobra), they hold a powerful religious significance in Hinduism and are often left alone. Other venomous snakes like the Russell's Viper, however, lack this significance and are often unfairly killed on sight.


Identification Methods:
  • South India: Yellowish-brown colour with prominent hood pattern.
  • North India: Much darker colouration with hood patterns possibly absent.
  • Slender body with smooth scales. Grows up to 3 - 5 feet on average
Sometimes confused with:
  • Indian Rat Snake


Common Krait (Bungarus caeruleus) - Venomous

The final member of the "Big 4", the Common Krait, has the most potent venom out of four.
This snake is nocturnal and identified by its slender body and white double-banded stripes that run along the body. This pattern typically starts about 1/3 of the way down the body.
Like the Russell's Viper, most bites happen at night when they are accidentally stepped on in rural / agricultural areas.
Unlike the rest of the Big 4, kraits don't have any audible or visual defense mechanism or warning.


Identification Methods:
  • Glossy black body with thin white double-band stripes.
  • Somewhat triangular shaped body with smooth scales.
Sometimes confused with:
  • Indian Wolf Snake



Colubrids (Colubridae)

The Colubrid family is the largest snake family in the world with members of this family being found on every continent except Antarctica. A majority of these snakes are harmless with only a few medically significant exceptions. Most are oviparous.

Indian Rat Snake (Pytas mucosa) - Harmless

Indian Rat Snakes are a diurnal species and among the most commonly seen snakes throughout India. These snakes are often mistaken to be cobras by the untrained eye but are much larger in length, averaging sizes of around 6 - 8 feet, making them among the largest nonvenomous species found in India.
Having rat snakes around your area is actually a benefit because not only are they excellent and natural rat control, but they'll actually scare off smaller venomous snakes from the area, making it a safer environment.



Indian Wolf Snake (Lycodon aulicus) - Harmless

The Indian Wolf Snake gets its name from a couple of fang-like front teeth that resemble the canines of a wolf. The wolf snake is often confused with the Common Krait as the appearance is similar with some key differences: the wolf snake's pattern always starts from the back of the neck, in contrast with the krait's, and has a more brown colour as opposed to the krait's solid black.
Wolf snakes are comfortable climbers and can be found looking for geckos between the bricks of houses or natural rocks and granite hills in the wild.




Bronzeback Tree Snake (Dendrelaphis bifrenalis) - Harmless

Bronzeback Tree Snakes are diurnal and arboreal hunters, mainly targeting frogs and lizards. At night, they can be seen sleeping in the peripheral of trees, their colouration providing good camoflauge.




Pythons (Pythonidae)

Pythons are large, snakes with a muscular build that constrict their prey by coiling around and suffocating them. All python species are nonvenomous and oviparous.
There are three species of python found in India.

Indian Rock Python (Python molurus) - Harmless

Indian Rock Pythons are a rare find in the outskirts of the city and are the only species of python found outside of the North Eastern states or the Andaman Islands. They are ambush hunters with an expansive diet, consisting primarily of locally abundant mammals.
Average adults are 10 - 16 feet in length.
